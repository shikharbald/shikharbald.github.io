How I think about system design interviews

The framework I wish I'd had going in — structure, trade-offs, and what interviewers actually care about.

I bombed my first few system design interviews. Not because I didn't know the material — I'd read all the right blog posts, studied distributed systems, could talk about CAP theorem. But I kept losing the thread. I'd go deep on caching before even agreeing on what the system needed to do. Interviewers would look politely glazed over.

After a lot of reps, I landed on a structure that works for me. It's not magic — it's basically just a way to avoid the common failure modes while giving you room to show genuine depth.

The actual failure modes

Before getting into structure, it helps to understand what goes wrong. In my experience, there are three main failure patterns:

  • Jumping to solutions — starting to design a database schema before understanding what scale you're building for
  • Breadth without depth — listing every component you've ever heard of without explaining why any of them are there
  • Avoiding trade-offs — treating every design decision as obviously correct rather than acknowledging what you're giving up

The framework below is basically just a way to avoid each of these.

The framework

1. Clarify requirements (5 minutes)

Don't touch the whiteboard yet. Ask questions. What does the system actually need to do? Who are the users? What's the scale — 1,000 users or 100 million? Is this read-heavy or write-heavy? What are the latency requirements?

Write down the answers visibly. This accomplishes two things: it forces you to actually understand the problem before solving it, and it shows the interviewer you can gather requirements — which is, y'know, a large part of the actual job.

2. Estimate scale, then do math

Once you have requirements, do rough napkin math. How many requests per second? How much data are you storing? These numbers will drive your architecture — there's no point discussing a write-ahead log if you're building for 100 users. 

Daily active users: 10M
Average requests per user per day: 20
Requests per second: 10M × 20 / 86,400 ≈ 2,300 RPS
Peak (assume 3x): ~7,000 RPS

The exact numbers don't matter. The exercise of thinking through them does.

3. Draw the high-level design

Now you can touch the whiteboard. Start simple: client, load balancer, app servers, database. Walk through a request end-to-end. Get alignment before going deeper.

4. Dive deep where it matters

Pick the interesting parts — usually whatever's hardest at the scale you estimated — and go deep. This is where you show real knowledge. Don't try to go deep on everything; you'll run out of time and it'll feel scattered.

What interviewers are actually evaluating

In my experience, the best interviewers aren't checking whether you arrived at the "correct" architecture. They're watching how you think. They want to see you handle ambiguity, reason about trade-offs, and communicate clearly. A candidate who says "I'd use Kafka here because we need to handle bursty writes and decouple the producer from the consumer, but the trade-off is operational complexity" is going to score better than one who says "Kafka, obviously" — even if both end up at the same design.

Design interviews are less about the destination and more about whether you'd be a good thinking partner on the journey.

That's the real unlock: stop trying to perform knowledge, and start actually thinking through the problem in the room with your interviewer.
